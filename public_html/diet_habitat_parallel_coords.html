About this page

How to use it

The dataset loads automatically once you close this panel — then switch between the Parallel Coordinates and Habitat ↔ Diet Flow tabs.

Parallel Coordinates draws every species as a line crossing four axes: body mass, habitat breadth, diet breadth, and the Ecological Specialization Index (ESI) — which is itself a formula of the two breadth axes next to it, so watching a line's path across those three right-hand axes shows the formula resolve in real time. Lines are colored by IUCN conservation status. Hover a line to see that species' photo, name, and its values across all four axes. Drag up or down on any axis to brush a range on it — only species that fall within the range on every currently-brushed axis stay visible, and the table below filters to match. Click an axis without dragging to clear just that axis's brush, or use Clear all selections to reset everything at once.

Habitat ↔ Diet Flow is a Sankey diagram: every species' primary habitat on the left, primary diet on the right, connected by a ribbon sized to how many species share that exact pairing. Hover a node to highlight its ribbons, or a ribbon for its exact count and conservation-status breakdown. Click a node or ribbon to filter the table below to just those species; use Clear selection to reset.

On both tabs, hover a chart element or a table row to highlight the other one — they're always linked in both directions. Hovering a species' name shows its photo; clicking the name opens its full species profile. Click any column header in a table to sort by it.

About this data

The species data on this page comes from BIRDBASE, a peer-reviewed global dataset of avian biogeography, conservation, ecology, and life-history traits — 78 traits for 11,589 species across 254 families, synthesized from 367 published sources spanning 1957–2025.

Şekercioğlu, Ç.H., Kittelberger, K.D., Mota, F.M.M. et al. BIRDBASE: A Global Dataset of Avian Biogeography, Conservation, Ecology and Life History Traits. Scientific Data (2025).

This page is a downstream retelling of that dataset, not a replacement for the source — see the full paper on nature.com for methodology, sources, and citation details.
