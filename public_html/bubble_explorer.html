    About this page

    How to use it

    The dataset loads automatically once you close this panel. Every bubble is a species — size is always body mass (larger = heavier bird, on an area-proportional scale). Choose what X axis, Y axis, and Color by mean, or click one of the story presets above the controls for a hand-picked view.

    Hovering a bubble shows its name. To find a specific species, type its common or scientific name into the search box beside the chart — picking a match highlights its bubble with a ring and opens a detail card below the search box with its current X/Y/ color values. Click a bubble, or the photo/name in its detail card, to open the full species profile.

    Some axes are categorical (primary diet, habitat, taxonomic order, migration status). Pick a categorical field for both axes and bubbles pack into a grid of cells — a "who eats what, and where" matrix. Pick one categorical and one numeric field for a beeswarm-style column layout. Two numeric fields give a classic scatter. Bubbles gently push apart when they'd overlap, so a crowded cluster can spill slightly past its exact position — that's expected, not an error.

    Species missing the data needed for the current X or Y are hidden, with a count shown above the chart. To keep colors readable, diet, habitat, and order are grouped into their four most common values plus "Other" — search for a species and check its detail card to see its exact value regardless of how it's grouped.

    About this data

    The species data on this page comes from BIRDBASE, a peer-reviewed global dataset of avian biogeography, conservation, ecology, and life-history traits — 78 traits for 11,589 species across 254 families, synthesized from 367 published sources spanning 1957–2025.

    Şekercioğlu, Ç.H., Kittelberger, K.D., Mota, F.M.M. et al. BIRDBASE: A Global Dataset of Avian Biogeography, Conservation, Ecology and Life History Traits. Scientific Data (2025).

    This page is a downstream retelling of that dataset, not a replacement for the source — see the full paper on nature.com for methodology, sources, and citation details.
